\section{Introduction}

Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) is a discrete-time optimization algorithm widely used in machine learning. Under the regime of a small learning rate $\eta$, its dynamics can be accurately modeled by a continuous-time stochastic differential equation (SDE), specifically the Overdamped Langevin Equation.

\section{The SGD Update Mechanism}

Consider an objective function $J(\theta)$ which is the mean of individual loss functions $J_i(\theta)$ over a dataset of size $n$:

$$
J(\theta) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} J_i(\theta).
$$

The standard SGD update at step $k$ for a randomly sampled index $i \in \{1, \dots, n\}$ is:

$$
\theta_{k+1} = \theta_k - \eta \nabla J_i(\theta_k)
$$

For simplicity, we abbreviated $i(k)$ as $i$.
The goal is to find the parameter $\theta$ that minimizes $J(\theta)$.

\subsection{Decomposition of the Gradient}

To understand the relationship between SGD and the true gradient descent path, we decompose the stochastic gradient into the full gradient and a noise term:

$$
\nabla J_i(\theta_k) = \nabla J(\theta_k) + \xi_k
$$

where $\xi_k = \nabla J_i(\theta_k) - \nabla J(\theta_k)$. 
Again, $\xi_k$ depends on the random index $i$ chosen at step $k$ and 
the parameter $\theta_k$, but we abbreviate the notation for simplicity.

The gradients $\nabla J_i(\theta_k)$ are i.i.d. given $\theta_k$ since 
they are computed from randomly sampled data points. If $n$ is large, 
the Law of Large Numbers implies that

$$ \nabla J(\theta_k) \approx \mathbb E [\nabla J_1(\theta_k) | \theta_k], $$

which allows us to treat $\xi_k$ as i.i.d. 
normal random variables with zero mean and some covariance 
$\beta>0$, i.e. 

$$\xi_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \beta I).$$

(Indeed, $\beta_k$ shall be dependent on $\theta_k$.) 
The update rule can be rewritten as:

$$
\theta_{k+1} - \theta_k = - \eta \nabla J(\theta_k) - \eta \sqrt{\beta} Z_k,$$

where $Z_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ is i.i.d. standard normal random variables.


\subsection{Continuous-Time Asymptotic (Langevin Limit)}

In the limit where $\eta \to 0$, we define a continuous-time process $\theta_t$ such that $\theta_k \approx \theta_{k\eta}$. 
We introduce a standard Brownian motion $W_t$ and using the fact that 

$$\sqrt{\eta} Z_k \approx W_{(k+1)\eta} - W_{k\eta},$$


the trajectory follows the Langevin equation:

$$
d\theta_t = -\nabla J(\theta_t) dt + \sqrt{\eta \beta} dW_t
$$

where $dW_t$ represents a Wiener process and the volatility term $\sqrt{\eta \beta}$ 
is the temperature parameter related to the learning rate and the covariance of the gradient noise $\xi$.
